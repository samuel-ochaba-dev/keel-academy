# Vague brief protocol

This is the most important reference in the skill. Most real-world prompts will be vague. "Build me a landing page for my SaaS" is far more common than a detailed creative brief.

When the brief is vague or missing, the default AI behavior is to produce the average of all copy ever seen for that page type. This protocol prevents that.

## The rule

**Never write generic copy. Either get specific information, or make specific assumptions.**

Generic copy is copy that could work for any product in the same category without changing a word. It is always wrong because it converts no one.

## Decision tree

When you receive a vague brief:

### 1. Can you ask questions?

If the context allows back-and-forth, ask these (max 3-5 at a time):

- What does your product actually DO in one sentence? (not what it "is")
- Who specifically is using it? (job title, situation, not "businesses")
- What were they doing before they had your product?
- What is the main reason people hesitate or say no?
- What is the ONE thing you want someone to do on this page?

These five questions give you enough to write specific copy. You do not need a brand book, tone guide, or persona document. You need the product, the person, the pain, the objection, and the action.

### 2. Can you infer from context?

If the prompt includes a product name, tech stack, industry, or any hints, use them aggressively:

- Product name suggests the category → write for that category's actual user
- Tech stack visible (React, Stripe, etc.) → the user is likely a developer
- Industry mentioned → use that industry's real vocabulary
- Competitor mentioned → differentiate against that competitor specifically
