# Copy principles

These are research-backed principles for writing website copy that converts. Use them as invisible structure, not visible labels.

## 1. Specificity converts

Research from 500+ A/B tests (Drexus, 2024) shows that changing 5-10 words can lift conversion by 20-35%. The winning variant is almost always more specific, not more clever.

- "Saves 4 hours/week" beats "Saves time"
- "Used by 12,000 marketing teams" beats "Trusted by thousands"
- "Get your report in 30 seconds" beats "Get instant results"

Rule: if you can add a number, a timeframe, a name, or a concrete outcome, do it.

## 2. Voice of customer beats invented language

Copy that mirrors the reader's own words outperforms copy written from inside the company (Copyhackers, 2014; Aberdeen Group).

When you have access to reviews, testimonials, or user language:

- Use their exact phrases as headlines
- Mirror their problem description in the opening
- Match their vocabulary level

When you do NOT have access:

- Imagine the search query that brought them here
- Write like you are answering that search query in conversation
- Use the words they would use to explain the problem to a friend

## 3. One page, one job

Every page has a single primary action. Every element on that page either supports that action or is noise.

- Homepage: get them to the right next page
- Landing page: get the conversion (signup, purchase, demo)
- Pricing page: get them to pick a plan
- Feature page: get them to try the feature
- Error page: get them back on track

Ask: "what is the ONE thing this page needs the reader to do?" Write every word in service of that one thing.

## 4. Benefits over features, outcomes over benefits

The hierarchy of persuasion:

- Feature (weakest): "AI-powered analytics"
- Benefit (better): "See what's working without digging through data"
- Outcome (strongest): "Know exactly where to spend next month's budget"

Always try to reach the outcome level. The outcome is the thing that changes in the reader's life.

## 5. Contrast creates urgency

Before/after framing is more persuasive than features/benefits because it activates loss aversion.

- "Stop guessing which campaigns work. Start knowing." (before/after)
- "Powerful campaign analytics for modern teams" (flat feature statement)

The first creates tension. The second sits there.

## 6. Front-load the value

Nielsen Norman Group research shows users scan in an F-pattern. The first 2 words of any line carry disproportionate weight.

- Put the most important word first in headlines
- Put the benefit before the mechanism
- Put the action before the explanation

Bad: "With our platform, you can track all your metrics in one place"
Good: "Track every metric. One dashboard."

## 7. Social proof is structure, not decoration

Effective social proof answers the reader's unspoken objection:

- "Will this work for someone like me?" → show a similar user
- "Is this legit?" → show quantity (10,000+ teams)
- "Will I get results?" → show a specific outcome with numbers
- "Is it hard to switch?" → show time-to-value ("Set up in 5 minutes")

Never use social proof generically. Each placement should answer a specific doubt.

## 8. Reduce cognitive load

Every word the reader has to process is a micro-cost. The copy with fewer words that says the same thing will always win.

- Cut adverbs ("very", "really", "incredibly", "seamlessly")
- Cut empty adjectives ("robust", "innovative", "powerful", "cutting-edge")
- Cut setup phrases ("It's worth noting that", "In order to", "We're proud to")
- Cut hedge words ("might", "could potentially", "helps you to")

What remains after cutting is the copy.

## 9. Write for the stage of awareness

Eugene Schwartz's 5 stages still hold:

1. **Unaware**: Lead with the problem, not the product
2. **Problem-aware**: Agitate the problem, introduce the category
3. **Solution-aware**: Differentiate from alternatives
4. **Product-aware**: Handle objections, prove claims
5. **Most aware**: Just give them the CTA and the deal

Matching copy to awareness stage is more important than any other variable.

## 10. The CTA is a promise, not a command

A/B tests consistently show (NextAfter, 2026; Unbounce):

- Specific CTAs outperform generic ones by 12-19% on average
- First-person CTAs ("Start my trial") outperform second-person ("Start your trial")
- Benefit-forward CTAs ("See it working") outperform action-only ("Start free trial") by up to 14.5%
- "Get" outperforms "Start" which outperforms "Submit"

The CTA should tell the reader what they GET, not what they have to DO.
